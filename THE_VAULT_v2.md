# MODULE 0: THE 15-MINUTE FAMILY BRIEFING

*Read this module NOW, while things are calm. It takes 15 minutes. It will save your family hours of chaos when something goes wrong.*

## SOP 0.1: The Family Emergency Protocol (FEP)

Most families have zero plan. When the power goes out, the phones die, or an evacuation order drops, everyone panics independently. This SOP fixes that.

### Step 1: Designate Roles (5 minutes)

Every household member over age 12 gets a role. Write these on the laminated card (Printable 0.1A).

| Role | Responsibility | Assigned To |
|------|---------------|-------------|
| **Commander** | Makes final decisions. Holds the Vault drive. | __________ |
| **Medic** | Owns the first aid kit. Executes Module 3 SOPs. | __________ |
| **Comms** | Owns the radio. Monitors emergency frequencies. | __________ |
| **Supply** | Manages food/water inventory. Runs the Calorie Calculator. | __________ |
| **Scout** | Gathers information. Checks perimeter. Makes supply runs. | __________ |

**If you live alone:** You are all roles. Prioritize Commander → Medic → Supply.

### Step 2: Establish Rally Points (5 minutes)

You need THREE rally points, memorized by every family member:

1. **Alpha Point (Home):** Your residence. Default meeting spot. If you are separated, everyone returns here first.
2. **Bravo Point (Local):** A location within walking distance (1-3 miles) that everyone knows — a church, school, relative's house. Used when Alpha is compromised (fire, flood, structural damage).
3. **Charlie Point (Regional):** A location 20-50 miles away, outside your immediate disaster zone. Used for regional evacuation. Must be reachable by at least two different routes.

**Write these on Card 0.1A. Every family member carries a copy.**

### Step 3: The 5-5-5 Communication Window (5 minutes)

When phones are down, you need a predictable check-in schedule so separated family members know when to attempt contact.

**The Rule:** Every day at **5:00 AM, 12:00 PM, and 5:00 PM**, every family member attempts to reach the others for exactly 5 minutes using whatever communication is available:
- Try cell phone first (networks may have intermittent capacity)
- Try text/SMS (uses less bandwidth than voice; often works when calls don't)
- Try FRS/GMRS radio on your pre-designated family channel
- If no contact is made, leave a physical signal at the Alpha rally point (chalk mark on the mailbox, note in a waterproof bag under the doormat — decide this NOW)

**The Signal Code:**
- One vertical line: "I am alive and safe"
- An arrow: "I have moved in this direction"
- A circle: "I need help at this location"
- An X: "Do not enter / danger"

## SOP 0.2: The "Go-Bag" Audit

*Most go-bags are packed once and forgotten. Batteries die. Medications expire. Water purification tablets degrade. This SOP is a quarterly audit.*

### The Go-Bag Checklist (Per Person)

**Tier 1: Non-Negotiable (Dies without these)**

- [ ] Water: 3L minimum (replace every 6 months)
- [ ] Water purification: Sawyer Squeeze OR 50 purification tablets (check expiry)
- [ ] Food: 3,600 calories minimum — energy bars, freeze-dried meals (check expiry)
- [ ] First aid kit: Tourniquet (CAT Gen 7), Israeli bandage, chest seal, gauze, medical tape, nitrile gloves, ibuprofen, Benadryl, personal medications (CHECK EXPIRY DATES)
- [ ] Fire: 3 methods — BIC lighter, ferrocerium rod, waterproof matches
- [ ] Shelter: Emergency bivvy OR lightweight tarp + 50ft paracord
- [ ] Light: Headlamp with fresh batteries + 1 spare set
- [ ] Knife: Fixed blade, full tang, 4-6 inch blade
- [ ] Documents: Copies of ID, insurance, medical records, emergency contacts in waterproof bag
- [ ] Cash: $200-500 in small bills ($1, $5, $10, $20). ATMs will not work.
- [ ] This Vault: Loaded on encrypted microSD card

**Tier 2: Force Multipliers (Significantly improves your situation)**

- [ ] Radio: Baofeng UV-5R or equivalent, pre-programmed with NOAA + local repeaters
- [ ] Navigation: Physical map of your county + compass (not a phone)
- [ ] Power: Small solar panel (10-20W) + battery bank (20,000mAh minimum)
- [ ] Clothing: Season-appropriate base layer, rain shell, extra socks (2 pairs), work gloves
- [ ] Hygiene: Travel toothbrush, soap, menstrual products, toilet paper (compressed), hand sanitizer
- [ ] Tools: Multi-tool (Leatherman or equivalent), duct tape (wrap around water bottle to save space), zip ties (10)
- [ ] Cordage: 100ft paracord (550 type III) beyond what's in Tier 1

**Tier 3: Extended Operations (If you might be out for 5+ days)**

- [ ] Shelter upgrade: Lightweight tent or hammock system
- [ ] Cook system: Compact stove + fuel + metal cup
- [ ] Advanced first aid: SAM splint, irrigation syringe, suture kit, QuikClot
- [ ] Fishing/trapping: Compact kit with hooks, line, snare wire
- [ ] Barter items: See Module 10

### Quarterly Audit Protocol

Every **January 1, April 1, July 1, October 1:**

1. Remove everything from the bag
2. Check every expiration date — replace anything within 3 months of expiry
3. Test every battery-powered device
4. Verify documents are current
5. Rotate cash if bills are damaged
6. Update medication prescriptions
7. Try on clothing (especially for children who outgrow gear)
8. Repack and weigh — should not exceed 20% of carrier's body weight

# MODULE 1: THE FIRST 72 HOURS — URGENT ACTION

*The first 72 hours of any crisis determine whether you stabilize or spiral. This module is pure execution — no theory.*

## DECISION TREE 1.1: What Just Happened?

*You've just experienced a sudden disruption. Work through this tree to determine your immediate action.*

```
START: Is there an immediate threat to life?
│
├── YES → Is it inside your home?
│         ├── YES → EVACUATE NOW. Go to Rally Point Bravo.
│         │         Skip to SOP 1.3 (Evacuation).
│         └── NO → Can you shelter in place safely?
│                   ├── YES → LOCKDOWN. Go to SOP 1.2.
│                   └── NO → EVACUATE. Go to SOP 1.3.
│
└── NO → Do you have power?
          ├── YES → Is it a localized event (your neighborhood)?
          │         ├── YES → Monitor news. Begin SOP 1.4 (Soft Prep).
          │         └── NO → Widespread event. Begin SOP 1.5 (Hard Prep).
          │
          └── NO → Do you have water pressure?
                    ├── YES → Fill ALL containers NOW (SOP 2.2).
                    │         Then begin SOP 1.5.
                    └── NO → Infrastructure is failing.
                              Begin SOP 1.5 IMMEDIATELY.
                              Water is your #1 priority (Module 2).
```

## SOP 1.2: Shelter-in-Place Lockdown

**Time to execute: 30 minutes**

This is not "wait and see." This is an active lockdown procedure.

### Phase 1: Secure (0-10 minutes)

1. **Lock all doors and windows.** Close all blinds/curtains.
2. **Account for all household members.** Use your FEP roster (SOP 0.1).
3. **Silence all phones** — vibrate only. Conserve battery.
4. **Turn off all unnecessary lights.** You do not want to advertise that your home has power if others don't.
5. **Fill every available container with water** — bathtubs, pots, pitchers, water bottles, washing machine. If water pressure exists, you have a limited window. **Average bathtub = 40 gallons. That's 13 days of water for one person.**
6. **Locate your go-bags.** Stage them at the front door. You are not leaving yet, but if you need to, you're ready.

### Phase 2: Assess (10-20 minutes)

7. **Turn on your battery-powered radio.** Scan NOAA Weather Radio frequencies:
   - 162.400, 162.425, 162.450, 162.475, 162.500, 162.525, 162.550 MHz
8. **Check your phone for emergency alerts.** If cell networks are up, download any available information NOW before they go down.
9. **Conduct a rapid inventory:**
   - Water: How many gallons do you have? (Divide by people × 1 gallon/day = days of supply)
   - Food: Use the Calorie Calculator (Tool B.1) or estimate: typical US pantry = 7-14 days for a family of 4
   - Medication: How many days of critical prescriptions remain?
   - Power: If you have a generator, how much fuel? (Typical generator burns 0.75 gal/hour at 50% load)
   - Light: How many flashlights/headlamps? Total battery life?

### Phase 3: Communicate (20-30 minutes)

10. **Contact your emergency contacts.** Use text messages first (lower bandwidth). Convey:
    - Your location
    - Your status (safe/injured/need help)
    - Your plan (staying/leaving)
11. **If you have neighbors you trust:** Make brief face-to-face contact. Share information. Do NOT share your supply levels.
12. **Begin a log.** Use the Situation Log template (Printable 1.2A). Record:
    - Time of event
    - What you know
    - What you've done
    - What you plan to do next
    - Resource levels

*This log is critical. In a multi-day crisis, your memory will fail you. Stress, sleep deprivation, and information overload will degrade your decision-making. The log is your external brain.*

## SOP 1.3: Emergency Evacuation

**Decision Point:** You evacuate when the risk of staying exceeds the risk of moving. Common triggers:
- Structural damage to your home
- Fire approaching
- Flooding/rising water
- Chemical/industrial spill nearby
- Civil unrest that is escalating toward your location
- Authorities ordering mandatory evacuation (take this seriously — they don't issue these lightly)

### The 10-Minute Evacuation Drill

*Practice this quarterly with your family. Time it. It should take under 10 minutes.*

1. **Announce: "We are leaving in 10 minutes."**
2. Each person grabs their go-bag (should be pre-staged)
3. **Commander** grabs: Vault drive, vehicle keys, physical maps, cash stash, weapons (if applicable)
4. **Medic** grabs: Full medical kit, all prescription medications, glasses/contacts
5. **Supply** grabs: Additional water (as much as vehicle can carry), cooler with perishables from fridge
6. **Comms** grabs: Radio, spare batteries, charging cables, solar panel
7. **All:** Put on sturdy shoes, weather-appropriate clothing, hat
8. **Commander:** Walk through home — turn off gas at meter, turn off main water valve, flip main breaker
9. **Load vehicle.** Go-bags first, then extras
10. **Leave via pre-planned primary route.** If blocked, take secondary route. Both should be memorized and physically mapped.

### Route Planning (Do This NOW, Not During the Crisis)

For each rally point (Bravo and Charlie), you need:
- **Primary route:** Fastest path using main roads
- **Secondary route:** Alternate path avoiding highways (highways become parking lots during mass evacuation)
- **Tertiary route:** Back roads, dirt roads, paths you could walk if driving is impossible

**Print these routes on physical maps. Highlight them. Laminate them. Put them in your go-bag.**

Mark on each route:
- Gas stations (may or may not be operational)
- Water sources (rivers, streams, lakes, public wells)
- Known hazards (flood zones, industrial facilities, military installations)
- Potential shelter points (churches, schools, fire stations, campgrounds)

### Vehicle Fuel Protocol

**The Half-Tank Rule:** Your vehicle should NEVER drop below half a tank. This is non-negotiable. Half a tank is your evacuation fuel. When you hit the halfway mark, you fill up. This is the single cheapest prep that most people ignore.

**Why:** During a crisis, gas stations will be either sold out, have lines measured in hours, or be physically dangerous to visit. Your vehicle's fuel tank is your mobile bug-out range.

**The Math:**
- Average sedan: 14-gallon tank, 30 mpg highway = 420 miles on a full tank, 210 on half
- Average SUV/truck: 20-gallon tank, 22 mpg = 440 miles full, 220 on half
- 220 miles at 45 mph average (accounting for traffic) = ~5 hours of driving

## SOP 1.4: Soft Prep (Power On, Localized Event)

*The power is on. This is a localized event. You are not in immediate danger, but something has changed — a natural disaster nearby, civil unrest in your city, a cyberattack that's disrupted services. This is your window to prepare before things potentially get worse.*

**Time to execute: 2-4 hours**

### Priority 1: Information (First 30 minutes)
1. Determine exactly what happened and what the projected timeline is
2. Download offline copies of critical information while you have internet
3. Charge ALL devices to 100% — phones, tablets, battery banks, radios, headlamps
4. Withdraw cash: $500-1,000 in small bills if ATMs are still operational

### Priority 2: Supplies (Next 60 minutes)
5. Make ONE supply run if stores are still accessible. Buy in this exact priority order:
   - Water (or containers to store water)
   - Batteries (AA, AAA, D, CR123A — whatever your devices use)
   - Fuel: Fill vehicle(s) to 100%. Fill gas cans if you have them.
   - Non-perishable food: Rice, beans, canned goods, peanut butter, honey
   - OTC medications: Ibuprofen, acetaminophen, anti-diarrheal, antihistamines, electrolyte packets
   - Hygiene: Bleach (unscented — also a water purifier), soap, toilet paper, trash bags

**Do NOT panic-buy. Be fast, decisive, and leave product for others. A community that collapses around you is worse than missing one item.**

### Priority 3: Coordination (Next 60 minutes)
6. Contact all emergency contacts. Align on plans.
7. Brief your neighbors. Offer to share information (not supplies).
8. Review your FEP (SOP 0.1) with your household.

### Priority 4: Hardening (Remaining time)
9. Stage go-bags at front door
10. Move important documents to waterproof container
11. Identify rooms with fewest windows (for shelter-in-place or security)
12. If you have a generator, test it. Now.
13. Review Modules 2-6 of this Vault relevant to your specific scenario

## SOP 1.5: Hard Prep (Power Down, Widespread Event)

*The power is out. It's not coming back soon. The scope is regional or larger. This is the real thing.*

**Time to execute: Ongoing operations**

### Hour 1: Immediate Actions
1. **DO NOT OPEN THE REFRIGERATOR.** A closed fridge maintains safe temps for 4 hours. A closed, full freezer maintains safe temps for 48 hours (24 hours if half-full). Every time you open it, you lose time.
2. Fill all water containers (if you have pressure — you have minutes, not hours, on municipal water after power fails because pumping stations run on electricity)
3. Account for all household members
4. Initiate 5-5-5 communication protocol
5. Deploy emergency lighting — use headlamps, not candles (fire risk is the #1 secondary killer in grid-down)

### Hours 2-6: Stabilization
6. Conduct full inventory using the Supply Calculator (Tool B.1)
7. Calculate your "runway" — how many days can you sustain with current supplies?
8. Set up your radio monitoring station (Module 4)
9. Establish a security watch rotation if you have multiple adults:
   - 4 hours on, 8 hours off
   - Watcher stays near a window with a view of approach routes
   - Watcher has a flashlight, radio, and a way to wake the household silently

### Hours 6-24: Organization
10. Establish water rationing: 1 gallon per person per day (½ gallon drinking, ½ gallon cooking/sanitation)
11. Cook perishable food FIRST — use your grill, camp stove, or improvised rocket stove
12. Establish a sanitation plan (SOP 2.8) — this becomes critical by Day 2
13. Make contact with neighbors. Begin sharing information. Identify skills in your immediate community.

### Hours 24-72: Adaptation
14. If power is not restored by Hour 24, shift to "extended operations" mindset. This is not a brief outage.
15. Begin water procurement operations (Module 2)
16. Establish community watch if neighbors are cooperative
17. Reduce caloric intake to 1,500 calories/day for adults if supplies are limited
18. Begin monitoring your mental state and your family's. Stress reactions will emerge. See Module 11.

# MODULE 2: WATER — PROCUREMENT, PURIFICATION & STORAGE

*Water is the non-negotiable resource. Everything else is secondary. You will die in 3 days without it. You will become cognitively impaired in 24 hours without it — making bad decisions that compound into fatal ones.*

## SOP 2.1: Emergency Water — The First Hour

**If you have municipal water pressure and it might fail:**

Fill EVERYTHING. Right now. In this order:
1. Bathtubs (plug first, obviously): ~40 gallons each
2. Every pot, pan, bowl, and pitcher in your kitchen
3. Washing machine (fill cycle, don't run): ~15-20 gallons
4. Water bottles, thermoses, Nalgenes
5. Trash bags inside boxes/bins (double-bag, fill, tie off): 10-15 gallons each
6. If you have a WaterBOB or similar bathtub liner, deploy it

**Hidden water in your home you already have:**
- Water heater tank: 30-80 gallons (turn off power/gas to heater first; drain from the valve at the bottom — it will be hot)
- Toilet tanks (NOT bowls): 1.5 gallons each. This water is clean if you don't use chemical cleaners in the tank.
- Ice cubes in freezer: ~2-4 lbs = ~1-2 quarts
- Canned goods liquid: Drain and save
- Pipe drainage: Open the highest faucet in the house, then collect from the lowest — gravity drains the pipes. Typically yields 2-5 gallons.

**Water you should NOT drink without purification:**
- Swimming pools/hot tubs (chemical treatment makes them non-potable for sustained use — but usable for washing and sanitation)
- Waterbed water (treated with chemicals; do not drink)
- Radiator fluid (toxic — never)
- Water with visible algae (can be purified, but extra steps needed)

## SOP 2.2: Water Source Identification & Ranking

Not all water is equal. Use this hierarchy:

### Tier 1: Safest (Still purify)
1. **Rainwater:** Cleanest natural source. Collect with tarps, buckets, rain gutters into clean containers. First 5 minutes of rain ("first flush") washes pollutants off your collection surface — divert this, then begin collecting.
2. **Deep well water:** If you have a well, you have a massive advantage. Manual hand pumps (like the Simple Pump or Bison) work without electricity. **Install one before you need it.**
3. **Spring water:** From a known, flowing spring. Springs are groundwater and generally clean.

### Tier 2: Moderate Risk (Must purify)
4. **Flowing streams/rivers:** Moving water is better than still water. Collect upstream of any settlement or agricultural area.
5. **Lake water:** Large bodies are generally cleaner than small ponds. Collect from the surface, away from shore.

### Tier 3: Last Resort (Must purify aggressively)
6. **Stagnant ponds:** High pathogen and algae risk. Pre-filter through cloth, then sand filter, then chemical or boil treatment.
7. **Urban runoff/drainage:** Only if truly desperate. Contains oil, chemicals, heavy metals. Multiple purification stages required.

### Finding Water When There's No Obvious Source

- **Follow animals and insects.** Bees are never more than 3 miles from water. Birds flying low in a consistent direction at dawn/dusk are heading to water.
- **Follow green vegetation.** In arid environments, the greenest line of vegetation marks underground water. Dig at the lowest point.
- **Dig a "seep well."** In a dry creek bed, dig 1-2 feet down. Water often sits just below the surface. Let it collect for an hour.
- **Morning dew collection.** Tie absorbent cloth around your ankles and walk through tall grass at dawn. Wring into a container. Yields ~1 cup per hour of walking — survival quantities, not comfort quantities.
- **Solar still.** Dig a hole 3 feet wide, 2 feet deep. Place a container in the center. Cover the hole with clear plastic sheeting, seal edges with dirt/rocks, and place a small stone in the center of the plastic so condensation drips into your container. Yields 1-3 cups per day depending on ground moisture and temperature. This is a last resort — calorie expenditure vs. water gain is marginal.

## SOP 2.3: The Purification Decision Tree

```
START: You have water from an unknown source.
│
├── Is the water visibly clear?
│   ├── YES → Go to PURIFICATION
│   └── NO → Go to PRE-FILTRATION first
│
PRE-FILTRATION:
│ 1. Let water sit for 1 hour (sediment settles)
│ 2. Pour through cloth (t-shirt, bandana) to remove large particles
│ 3. If still cloudy, build an improvised sand filter (SOP 2.4)
│ 4. Proceed to PURIFICATION
│
PURIFICATION: Choose your method based on what you have:
│
├── Do you have FIRE/FUEL?
│   └── YES → BOIL. Rolling boil for 1 minute (3 min above 6,500 ft).
│             This is the gold standard. Kills all pathogens.
│
├── Do you have a COMMERCIAL FILTER (Sawyer, LifeStraw, Katadyn)?
│   └── YES → Filter per manufacturer instructions.
│             NOTE: Most filters do NOT remove viruses.
│             If viral contamination is suspected (human sewage
│             in the water source), filter THEN chemically treat.
│
├── Do you have UNSCENTED LIQUID BLEACH (6-8.25% sodium hypochlorite)?
│   └── YES → Add per this table:
│             Clear water: 6 drops per gallon (8.25%) or 8 drops (6%)
│             Cloudy water: 12 drops per gallon (8.25%) or 16 drops (6%)
│             Stir and let stand 30 minutes.
│             Should have slight chlorine smell. If not, repeat dose
│             and wait 15 more minutes.
│
├── Do you have WATER PURIFICATION TABLETS (Aquatabs, Potable Aqua)?
│   └── YES → Follow package instructions exactly.
│             Typical: 1 tablet per liter, wait 30 minutes.
│             Double dose for cloudy water.
│
├── Do you have IODINE (2% tincture)?
│   └── YES → 5 drops per quart (clear) or 10 drops (cloudy).
│             Wait 30 minutes. NOT safe for pregnant women,
│             people with thyroid conditions, or long-term use.
│
└── You have NOTHING?
    └── UV from sunlight (SODIS method):
        Fill clear PET plastic bottles (soda bottles).
        Lay in direct sunlight for 6 hours (full sun)
        or 2 days (cloudy). Kills bacteria and viruses.
        Does NOT remove chemical contaminants.
```

## SOP 2.4: Building an Improvised Sand Filter

**Materials:** 2-liter bottle (or similar container), gravel, coarse sand, fine sand, charcoal (from your fire — NOT briquettes which contain chemicals), cloth.

**Assembly (bottom to top):**
1. Cut the bottom off the bottle. Turn it upside down (cap end = spout).
2. Place cloth or coffee filter at the cap opening
3. Layer 1 (bottom): 2 inches of fine gravel
4. Layer 2: 2 inches of coarse sand
5. Layer 3: 4 inches of crushed charcoal
6. Layer 4: 4 inches of fine sand
7. Layer 5 (top): 2 inches of fine gravel

**Operation:**
- Pour water slowly into the top
- Collect from the spout
- First 2-3 passes will be cloudy — discard or re-filter
- **THIS DOES NOT REPLACE PURIFICATION.** This is pre-filtration only. You must still boil or chemically treat the filtered water.
- Replace charcoal layer every 50 gallons or when output becomes cloudy despite multiple passes

## SOP 2.5: Long-Term Water Storage

**Before a crisis (do this now):**
- Store 1 gallon per person per day, minimum 14-day supply
- For a family of 4: 56 gallons minimum
- Use food-grade containers only (HDPE plastic, marked with recycling symbol #2)
- Add 1/8 teaspoon of unscented bleach per gallon for storage
- Store in cool, dark location away from chemicals and gasoline
- Replace stored water every 12 months (mark the date on the container)
- **Best containers:** 7-gallon Aqua-Tainers (stackable, spigot), 55-gallon drums (need a pump), WaterBricks (interlocking, portable)

# MODULE 3: FIELD MEDICINE & TRAUMA

*You are not a doctor. This module does not make you one. It makes you a capable first responder who can keep someone alive until professional help arrives — or, if professional help is not coming, gives you the best chance of managing common emergencies.*

## SOP 3.1: The MARCH Protocol — Tactical Trauma Response

*This is the standard military trauma protocol adapted for civilians. Memorize the acronym. Work it in order. Do NOT skip steps.*

### M — Massive Hemorrhage

**The single biggest killer in trauma is uncontrolled bleeding.** Treat this FIRST.

**Decision Point: Is the bleeding life-threatening?**
- Blood is spurting (arterial)
- Blood is flowing steadily and won't stop with direct pressure
- Blood has soaked through clothing
- There is a pool of blood larger than a dinner plate
- The casualty has lost an arm or leg (traumatic amputation)

**If YES — Tourniquet:**

1. Apply a commercial tourniquet (CAT Gen 7 or SOF-T Wide) 2-3 inches above the wound, NEVER on a joint
2. Pull the strap tight, then twist the windlass until bleeding stops
3. Secure the windlass in the clip
4. **Write the TIME on the tourniquet or the casualty's forehead** with a marker. This is critical for medical handoff.
5. Do NOT remove the tourniquet. Do NOT loosen it "to check." That is how people bleed out.

**Common tourniquet errors that kill people:**
- **Too loose:** A tourniquet that doesn't fully occlude arterial flow actually INCREASES bleeding by trapping venous blood. If it's not agonizingly tight, it's not tight enough.
- **Too low:** Place it as high on the limb as possible. "High and tight" is the mantra.
- **Removing too early:** A tourniquet can stay on for 2+ hours without causing permanent damage. Removing it in the field when you don't have blood products or surgical capability is almost always wrong.

**If NO — Wound Packing & Direct Pressure:**

1. Expose the wound (cut away clothing)
2. Pack gauze DIRECTLY INTO the wound cavity — push it in with your fingers
3. Apply firm, constant pressure for a minimum of 3 minutes (time it — it feels much longer than 3 minutes)
4. If blood soaks through, DO NOT remove the gauze — add more on top and keep pressure
5. Once bleeding is controlled, apply a pressure bandage (Israeli bandage, ACE wrap) over the packed wound

### A — Airway

**Is the person conscious and talking?** → Airway is open. Move to R.

**If unconscious:**
1. Tilt head back, lift chin (head-tilt chin-lift maneuver)
2. Look in the mouth for obstructions — blood, vomit, teeth, food. Sweep with a finger if you see something.
3. If the airway won't stay open, place them in the **recovery position:** Roll them onto their side, top knee forward for stability, hand under cheek. This keeps the tongue from blocking the airway and allows fluids to drain.
4. If you have a nasopharyngeal airway (NPA), lubricate it and insert it gently into one nostril until the flange rests against the nostril. This keeps the airway open in an unconscious patient.

### R — Respiration

**Is the chest rising and falling? Can you hear breathing?**

**Penetrating chest wound (gunshot, stab, impalement):**
1. If you hear sucking or see bubbles at the wound → this is a "sucking chest wound" (open pneumothorax)
2. Apply a chest seal (Hyfin or equivalent) — sticky side to skin, covering the wound completely
3. If you don't have a chest seal: use any airtight material (plastic bag, credit card, duct tape) and tape on THREE sides only. The fourth side acts as a flutter valve — lets air out, prevents it from being sucked in.
4. If the patient deteriorates after sealing (gets worse, not better), they may have a tension pneumothorax. **Burp** the seal (lift one corner briefly to release trapped air) and reseal.

**Broken ribs without penetration:**
- Stabilize with padding. Do NOT wrap the chest tightly — this restricts breathing and can cause pneumonia.
- Position the patient sitting upright or leaning toward the injured side.

### C — Circulation (Shock Prevention)

Even after bleeding is controlled, shock can kill. **Shock = not enough blood circulating to sustain organs.**

**Signs of shock:**
- Pale, cold, clammy skin
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Confusion, anxiety, combativeness
- Rapid shallow breathing
- Nausea/vomiting
- Thirst

**Treatment:**
1. Lay the patient flat. Elevate legs 6-12 inches (if no spinal injury suspected).
2. **Keep them warm.** Hypothermia dramatically accelerates shock. Wrap in blankets, sleeping bags, emergency bivvys. Insulate from the ground (this is where most heat loss occurs).
3. Do NOT give food or water if surgical care might be available (aspiration risk). If you are certain no surgical care is coming, small sips of water are acceptable after 1 hour if the patient is conscious and not vomiting.
4. Keep talking to them. Calm, reassuring voice. Their stress response is already maxed — your job is to be the calm in the chaos.

### H — Head Injury & Hypothermia

**Head injury signs:**
- Unequal pupil sizes (one big, one small) → CRITICAL — this suggests brain swelling
- Clear fluid from ears or nose → possible skull fracture
- Progressively worsening consciousness (alert → confused → unconscious)
- Repeated vomiting after head trauma

**Management:**
- Do NOT let them sleep for the first 4 hours — check on them every 15 minutes
- Keep head elevated 30 degrees
- If vomiting, recovery position to prevent aspiration
- There is very little you can do for a serious brain injury in the field. Your job is to keep them alive and prevent secondary injury (keep them warm, keep the airway open, keep them calm).

**Hypothermia prevention:**
- Remove wet clothing
- Insulate from the ground (this is where 80% of conductive heat loss occurs)
- Wrap in layers — sleeping bag, blankets, emergency bivvy
- If conscious, give warm (not hot) sweet drinks
- Do NOT rub extremities or apply direct heat to arms/legs — this can cause cardiac arrest by sending cold blood back to the core too quickly ("afterdrop")

## SOP 3.2: Common Field Medical Emergencies

### Burns

**Severity:**
- **First degree (red, no blisters):** Cool running water 10 minutes. Aloe. OTC pain relief. No further treatment needed.
- **Second degree (blisters):** Cool water 10-20 minutes. Do NOT pop blisters. Cover with non-stick sterile dressing. OTC pain relief. Watch for infection.
- **Third degree (white/charred, no pain in burned area):** This is beyond field treatment. Cool with water to stop burning process. Cover loosely with clean cloth. Treat for shock. Get to medical care.

**The Rule:** Cool the burn with water, NOT ice. Ice causes frostbite on burned tissue.

**Burns over >15% body surface area in adults (or >10% in children)** are life-threatening emergencies. The "Rule of Nines":
- Head = 9%
- Each arm = 9%
- Chest = 18%
- Back = 18%
- Each leg = 18%
- Groin = 1%

### Fractures & Sprains

**You cannot reliably distinguish a fracture from a bad sprain without an X-ray. Treat all as fractures.**

1. Immobilize the joint ABOVE and BELOW the injury
2. Use a SAM splint (moldable aluminum), or improvise with sticks, rolled magazines, cardboard
3. Pad between the splint and skin
4. Secure with bandages, strips of cloth, or duct tape (not directly on skin)
5. Check circulation below the splint every 30 minutes — can they feel their fingers/toes? Are they warm? Is the skin color normal?
6. RICE: Rest, Ice (if available), Compression, Elevation
7. Ibuprofen for pain and inflammation (400-800mg every 6-8 hours with food)

### Wound Infection

In a grid-down scenario without antibiotics, wound infection becomes a leading killer. **Prevention is everything.**

**Cleaning a wound:**
1. Wash your hands thoroughly (or use gloves)
2. Irrigate the wound with clean water — use pressure (fill a water bottle, poke a small hole in the cap, squeeze directly into the wound). Volume matters more than what you use. Use at least 500mL.
3. Remove any visible debris with clean tweezers
4. Apply thin layer of antibiotic ointment (if available)
5. Cover with sterile dressing
6. Change dressing daily (or whenever it becomes wet/dirty)

**Signs of infection (watch for these starting Day 2):**
- Increasing redness spreading outward from wound
- Swelling that's getting worse, not better
- Warmth around the wound
- Pus (yellow/green discharge)
- Red streaks radiating from wound toward the heart → **THIS IS AN EMERGENCY.** Red streaks indicate the infection has entered the lymphatic system (lymphangitis) and sepsis may follow.
- Fever, chills, malaise

**Field treatment of early infection (no medical care available):**
- Clean the wound aggressively — reopen if necessary, irrigate thoroughly
- Apply warm compresses (warm wet cloth) for 20 minutes, 3-4 times daily — this increases blood flow and immune response
- If you have antibiotics (fish antibiotics are identical to human antibiotics and available without prescription in many areas — research before a crisis):
  - Amoxicillin 500mg every 8 hours for skin infections
  - Ciprofloxacin 500mg every 12 hours for broader coverage
  - Metronidazole 500mg every 8 hours for anaerobic infections (dirty wounds, animal bites)
- Honey (raw, unpasteurized) has genuine antimicrobial properties and has been used for wound treatment for thousands of years. Apply directly to the wound and cover.

### Dental Emergencies

Dental pain in a grid-down scenario is miserable and can lead to systemic infection.

- **Toothache:** Clove oil (eugenol) applied directly to the tooth is a legitimate analgesic and antiseptic. Ibuprofen 600mg + acetaminophen 500mg together is as effective as many prescription pain medications.
- **Lost filling:** Temporary filling material (DenTemp, available at pharmacies) or softened candle wax as a temporary seal.
- **Knocked-out tooth:** Place in milk (or saliva) and get to a dentist within 1 hour for reimplantation. In a grid-down scenario, this is unlikely — rinse the socket, pack with gauze, bite down.
- **Abscess (swelling, severe pain, fever):** This is a systemic infection risk. Warm salt water rinses (1 tsp salt per 8oz warm water) every 2 hours. Amoxicillin 500mg every 8 hours if available. If swelling spreads to the throat or under the jaw, this can become a life-threatening airway emergency.

## SOP 3.3: Medication Management in Extended Emergencies

### Building a Crisis Medication Kit

**OTC Essentials (buy these NOW, rotate annually):**

| Medication | Use | Dose | Quantity |
|-----------|-----|------|----------|
| Ibuprofen 200mg | Pain, inflammation, fever | 2-4 tabs every 6-8h | 500 tablets |
| Acetaminophen 500mg | Pain, fever (liver-safe alt) | 1-2 tabs every 6h | 500 tablets |
| Diphenhydramine 25mg (Benadryl) | Allergic reactions, sleep aid | 1-2 tabs every 6h | 200 tablets |
| Loperamide 2mg (Imodium) | Diarrhea (prevents dehydration) | 2 tabs first dose, then 1 after each loose stool, max 8/day | 200 tablets |
| Oral Rehydration Salts | Dehydration from illness | Per packet instructions | 50 packets |
| Hydrocortisone cream 1% | Rashes, insect bites, skin irritation | Apply thin layer 2-3x daily | 10 tubes |
| Triple antibiotic ointment | Wound infection prevention | Apply to clean wound daily | 10 tubes |
| Antacid tablets | Heartburn, stomach acid | Per package | 200 tablets |
| Electrolyte packets | Dehydration, heat illness | Mix with water per instructions | 50 packets |

**Prescription Medication Strategy:**
- Talk to your doctor about getting a 90-day supply of all critical medications
- Many pharmacies offer auto-refill — set it to refill at Day 60 so you always have a 30-day buffer
- Store medications in cool, dark, dry location. Most medications remain effective well past their expiration date (a major FDA study found 90% of medications were still good 5-15 years past expiration). Exception: liquid antibiotics, nitroglycerin, insulin, and epinephrine degrade faster.
- **EpiPens:** If anyone in your household has severe allergies, you need minimum 4 EpiPens (they expire, and you might need more than one dose). Expired EpiPens still contain epinephrine — better to use an expired one than none at all.

### Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) — DIY Recipe

If you can't get commercial ORS packets:
- 1 liter of clean water
- 6 level teaspoons of sugar
- ½ level teaspoon of salt

Mix thoroughly. Sip slowly. This replaces electrolytes lost through diarrhea, vomiting, or sweating. **Diarrhea kills more people in disaster scenarios than trauma** — primarily through dehydration.

# MODULE 4: COMMUNICATION WHEN THE GRID IS DOWN

*Your phone is dead or the towers are down. How do you know what's happening? How do you coordinate with others? Communication is the difference between organized response and blind panic.*

## SOP 4.1: The Communication Hierarchy

When modern communications fail, work down this ladder:

### Tier 1: Cell Phone (Modified Use)
- Switch to airplane mode, then enable Wi-Fi only (saves battery)
- Text messages use far less bandwidth than voice calls — a text may get through when a call won't
- If towers are overloaded, try texting at off-peak times (2-5 AM)
- Conserve battery: lower brightness, close all apps, disable Bluetooth/location services
- **Battery math:** Modern smartphone at 100% in airplane mode with minimal use = 3-5 days

### Tier 2: FRS/GMRS Radio (Family Radio)
- Range: 1-5 miles (realistic, despite manufacturer claims of 25+)
- No license required for FRS; GMRS technically requires an FCC license ($35, covers whole family for 10 years) but in a genuine emergency, nobody is checking
- **Pre-designate a family channel and CTCSS tone** — write it on your FEP card
- Recommended channels: Channel 1 = family, Channel 20 = emergency calling channel (widely used convention)
- Battery life: ~20 hours typical with alkaline batteries. Carry spares.

### Tier 3: Ham Radio (Amateur Radio)
- Range: Local to GLOBAL depending on frequency and conditions
- **Requires an FCC license to transmit (Technician class minimum)**. Get this NOW — the test is 35 multiple-choice questions, and the study material is free online.
- In a declared emergency, FCC regulations allow anyone to transmit on any frequency if life or property is in immediate danger
- **Recommended starter radio:** Baofeng UV-5R ($25-30). Program it with your local repeater frequencies and NOAA weather frequencies BEFORE a crisis.
- Local repeaters extend your range dramatically — a 5-watt handheld through a repeater can reach 50+ miles
- **Join your local ARES/RACES group** (Amateur Radio Emergency Service). These are the people who provide emergency communication when everything else fails. They train regularly and have infrastructure in place.

### Tier 4: Passive Monitoring
- **NOAA Weather Radio:** Battery-powered, always broadcasting. 7 frequencies (see SOP 1.2). Buy a dedicated NOAA radio with SAME alerts — $20-30 at any electronics store.
- **AM/FM Radio:** Local stations often have emergency generators and will broadcast critical information. AM radio travels further than FM, especially at night.
- **Shortwave Radio:** Receive international broadcasts and ham radio traffic from thousands of miles away. Good for understanding the scope of a widespread event.

### Tier 5: Non-Electronic
- **Physical signals:** Mirror signals (visible 10+ miles in sunlight), smoke signals (three fires in a triangle = international distress signal), ground-to-air signals (SOS spelled with rocks/logs, visible to aircraft)
- **Runners:** Designate someone to physically carry messages between groups. This is how armies communicated for millennia. Slow, but reliable.
- **Dead drops:** Pre-arranged locations where you leave written messages for specific people. The message is in a waterproof container at a specific spot. Only works if pre-arranged.

---

## SOP 4.2: Setting Up a Base Station

If you have a ham radio (even a cheap Baofeng), you can set up a community communication hub.

**Equipment:**
- Radio: Baofeng UV-5R or better (Yaesu FT-60R for more reliability)
- Antenna: Upgrade from the stock antenna to a Nagoya NA-771 ($10) for dramatically better range
- Power: 12V battery (car battery works) with adapter. A car battery can power a 5W radio for days.
- Log book: Record all communications — time, frequency, caller, message content
- Solar panel (20W minimum) to keep your battery charged

**Operating Procedure:**
1. Monitor before transmitting — listen for 30 seconds to make sure the frequency is clear
2. Keep transmissions short — state your callsign (if licensed), your location, and your message
3. Use plain language, not codes (unless communicating with trained operators who know the codes)
4. Establish a regular check-in schedule with other stations — "Every day at 0800 and 2000 on [frequency]"
5. Log everything — this is your intelligence feed

# MODULE 5: FOOD SECURITY — THE 90-DAY RUNWAY

## SOP 5.1: The Calorie-Day Calculator

*This is the most important calculation you will make in an extended crisis. It tells you exactly how long you can survive on your current food supply.*

### The Formula

**(Total Calories in Storage) ÷ (Number of People × Daily Calorie Need) = Days of Survival**

### Daily Calorie Needs (Crisis Level — below normal, above starvation)

| Person | Active (physical labor) | Sedentary (sheltering) |
|--------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Adult Male | 2,200 | 1,600 |
| Adult Female | 1,800 | 1,400 |
| Child (6-12) | 1,600 | 1,200 |
| Child (1-5) | 1,200 | 1,000 |
| Elderly (65+) | 1,600 | 1,200 |
| Pregnant/Nursing | 2,200 | 1,800 |

### Quick Calorie Reference for Common Storage Foods

| Food | Calories per Unit | Shelf Life |
|------|-------------------|------------|
| White rice (1 lb) | 1,650 | 25-30 years (sealed) |
| Dried beans (1 lb) | 1,500 | 25-30 years (sealed) |
| Rolled oats (1 lb) | 1,750 | 25-30 years (sealed) |
| Peanut butter (16 oz jar) | 2,600 | 2 years |
| Honey (1 lb) | 1,380 | Indefinite |
| Canned tuna (5 oz) | 200 | 3-5 years |
| Canned vegetables (15 oz) | 100-200 | 3-5 years |
| Canned fruit (15 oz) | 200-350 | 3-5 years |
| Pasta (1 lb) | 1,680 | 2 years (8-20 sealed) |
| Powdered milk (1 lb) | 1,600 | 20 years (sealed) |
| Sugar (1 lb) | 1,725 | Indefinite |
| Salt (1 lb) | 0 (essential electrolyte) | Indefinite |
| Cooking oil (1 cup) | 1,900 | 1-2 years |
| Freeze-dried meals | ~250-400 per serving | 25 years |

### Example Calculation

**Family of 4** (2 adults, 2 children aged 8 and 10), sheltering in place:
- Daily need: 1,600 + 1,400 + 1,200 + 1,200 = **5,400 calories/day**

**Pantry inventory:**
- 20 lbs rice = 33,000 cal
- 10 lbs beans = 15,000 cal
- 5 lbs pasta = 8,400 cal
- 10 cans tuna = 2,000 cal
- 20 cans vegetables = 3,000 cal
- 5 jars peanut butter = 13,000 cal
- 5 lbs oats = 8,750 cal
- Misc canned goods = ~5,000 cal

**Total: ~88,150 calories**
**88,150 ÷ 5,400 = 16.3 days**

*Most families think they have a month of food. Most have 2-3 weeks at reduced intake. Do this math NOW, while you have time to fill gaps.*

## SOP 5.2: Consumption Protocol

**Phase 1 (Days 1-3): Eat the perishables**
- Fresh food, refrigerator contents, freezer contents (in that order)
- Cook all raw meat before it spoils — grilled/smoked meat lasts longer
- Eat dairy, eggs, and produce first

**Phase 2 (Days 4-14): Dry goods and canned foods**
- Rotate through canned goods (protein first: tuna, chicken, beans)
- Cook rice and beans in bulk (saves fuel)
- Ration snack foods — they're calorie-dense morale boosters

**Phase 3 (Days 14+): Deep storage and foraging**
- Shift to long-term storage foods (freeze-dried, #10 cans)
- Begin supplementing with foraged foods, fishing, trapping if possible
- Reduce caloric intake to minimum sustainable levels if supply is running low

**Meal Planning Rules:**
1. ONE hot meal per day. This is a morale imperative, not just nutrition.
2. Eat the most calorie-dense foods when doing physical labor
3. Never skip breakfast — it sets your metabolic tone for the day
4. Drink water with every meal — dehydration mimics hunger, causing you to eat more than necessary

## SOP 5.3: Emergency Gardening — Fast Calories

If the crisis extends beyond your stored food, you need to grow calories.

**Fastest-producing crops (can produce food in 20-60 days):**

| Crop | Days to Harvest | Calories/sq ft/season | Notes |
|------|----------------|----------------------|-------|
| Radishes | 20-30 | Low (but fast) | Good for morale, vitamins |
| Lettuce/greens | 30-45 | Low | Vitamins, minerals |
| Bush beans | 50-60 | Moderate | Protein source |
| Potatoes | 70-90 | HIGH (~100 cal/lb) | Plant cut pieces with "eyes" |
| Sweet potatoes | 90-120 | HIGH | Leaves are also edible |
| Squash | 50-60 | Moderate-High | Stores well |
| Corn | 70-90 | HIGH | Calorie-dense, stores as dried |

**The Survival Garden Priority:** Plant POTATOES, BEANS, and SQUASH. This is the "Three Sisters" adapted for survival — carbs, protein, and vitamins. Add greens for micronutrients.

**No seeds? No problem:**
- Potato eyes from pantry potatoes → plant 4 inches deep
- Garlic cloves → plant individual cloves
- Green onion roots → place in water, they regrow indefinitely
- Tomato seeds from a fresh tomato → dry and plant
- Sweet potato → suspend in water until it sprouts slips, then plant the slips

# MODULE 6: POWER GENERATION & ENERGY MANAGEMENT

## SOP 6.1: The Power Triage

*You cannot power everything. You must decide what matters.*

### Critical Loads (Must Power)

| Device | Watts (approx) | Daily Hours Needed | Daily Wh |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|----------|
| Medical device (CPAP, etc.) | 30-60W | 8h | 240-480 |
| Phone charging | 10W | 2h | 20 |
| Radio (receive) | 2W | 8h | 16 |
| LED lighting | 5-10W | 5h | 25-50 |
| Laptop (for Vault access) | 45-65W | 1h | 45-65 |
| **TOTAL DAILY NEED** | | | **~350-630 Wh** |

### Non-Critical Loads (Disconnect)

- Refrigerator (exception: first 24-48h to preserve perishables)
- TV, gaming systems, entertainment
- Clocks, standby electronics ("phantom loads" — up to 10% of home power)
- Electric stove/oven (use camp stove, grill, or fire instead)
- Washer/dryer
- HVAC (exception: medical necessity for extreme temperatures)

## SOP 6.2: Generator Operations

**SAFETY FIRST — Carbon Monoxide Kills:**
- NEVER run a generator indoors, in a garage, or within 20 feet of any opening (window, door, vent)
- CO is odorless and invisible. It kills in minutes. Every year people die running generators inside during storms.
- Buy a battery-powered CO detector for every floor of your home ($20-30 each). Do this NOW.

**Fuel Management:**
- Typical portable generator (3,500W): 0.5-0.75 gallons per hour at 50% load
- 5-gallon gas can = ~7-10 hours of runtime
- **Stored gasoline degrades.** Add fuel stabilizer (Sta-Bil or equivalent) when you fill your cans. Stabilized gas lasts 12-24 months. Unstabilized gas degrades in 3-6 months.
- **Rotation protocol:** Use the oldest gas in your vehicles, replace with fresh. Mark each can with the date filled.

**Connection:**
- **NEVER backfeed your home's electrical panel by plugging a generator into a wall outlet.** This sends power back into the grid lines and can electrocute utility workers trying to restore power. It's also illegal.
- Use a transfer switch ($200-500 professionally installed) for safe whole-home connection
- OR run individual extension cords from the generator to devices. Use heavy-gauge outdoor extension cords (12 AWG minimum for runs over 25 feet).

**Operational Protocol:**
- Run the generator on a schedule, not continuously. 4 hours on, 8 hours off.
- During "on" periods: charge all batteries, run medical devices, cook, pump water if applicable
- During "off" periods: operate on battery power only
- This extends your fuel supply by 3x

## SOP 6.3: Solar Power Basics

**Minimum Viable Solar Setup:**
- 100W solar panel ($80-150)
- 20A charge controller ($20-40)
- 12V deep-cycle battery (marine/RV battery, $80-150)
- 300W pure sine wave inverter ($30-50) — converts 12V DC to 120V AC for household devices
- **Total cost: ~$210-390**

**Daily output (realistic):**
- 100W panel in direct sun: ~400-600 Wh per day (assuming 4-6 peak sun hours, depending on location and season)
- This is enough to run: LED lighting, phone charging, radio, and a laptop
- NOT enough for: refrigerator, heating, cooking, power tools

**Optimization:**
- Re-orient panel every 2 hours to track the sun (increases output 20-30%)
- Clean dust/debris off panel daily
- Shade on even a small portion of the panel drastically reduces output (a single leaf can cut output by 30-50%)
- In winter, tilt panels steeper (latitude + 15 degrees); in summer, flatter (latitude - 15 degrees)
- Snow reflects light — panels next to snow-covered ground produce more than you'd expect

**Scaling Up:**
- 200W ($150-300) handles a small refrigerator for 6-8 hours/day
- 400W ($300-600) provides moderate daily power including intermittent power tools
- 800W+ ($600-1200) approaches household coverage for critical needs

**Battery Bank Sizing:**
- You want enough battery to get through 2 days without sun (cloudy days)
- Daily need (from SOP 6.1): ~500 Wh = ~42 Ah at 12V
- 2-day reserve: 84 Ah at 12V
- Lead-acid batteries should only be discharged to 50% → you need 168 Ah of rated capacity
- One marine deep-cycle battery (Group 27, ~100 Ah) gets you through 1 day. Two batteries = comfortable margin.

# MODULE 7: SHELTER — HARDENING, HEATING & COOLING

## SOP 7.1: Home Hardening Checklist

### Against Storm/Wind Damage
- [ ] Identify your "safe room" — interior room, lowest floor, no windows. Bathroom or closet is ideal.
- [ ] Plywood for window boarding: Pre-cut to fit each window. Label each piece. Store with screws and a cordless drill.
- [ ] Secure outdoor objects that become projectiles: Patio furniture, trash cans, grills
- [ ] Know how to shut off: Gas main (wrench taped to the meter), water main, electrical main breaker
- [ ] Clear gutters and drains to prevent water intrusion
- [ ] Trim dead branches within falling distance of the house

### Against Intrusion (Extended Crisis)
- [ ] Reinforce exterior doors: 3-inch screws in strike plates (replacing the standard 3/4-inch screws that come from the factory — this is the single most cost-effective security upgrade)
- [ ] Door barricade bars for sliding doors
- [ ] Motion-activated solar lights on all approach paths
- [ ] Thorny bushes under ground-floor windows (natural barrier)
- [ ] Dog: The most effective alarm system ever invented
- [ ] Safe room designation with lock, communication device, and supplies for 24 hours

## SOP 7.2: Heating Without Power

**The danger:** In northern climates, an unheated home reaches dangerous temperatures within 24-48 hours in winter. Hypothermia is a real threat, especially for elderly and children.

**Strategy: Heat one room, not the whole house.**

1. Choose the smallest interior room. South-facing if possible.
2. Seal doorways with towels, blankets, or plastic sheeting to trap heat.
3. Cover windows with blankets or bubble wrap (effective insulator).
4. All household members sleep in the same room — body heat matters.

**Heat sources (safest to least safe):**

| Source | Output | Fuel | Safety Notes |
|--------|--------|------|-------------|
| Wood stove/fireplace | High | Wood | Must have working chimney. Crack a window 1 inch for oxygen. |
| Kerosene heater | High | Kerosene | Ventilation critical. CO risk. Use CO detector. |
| Propane buddy heater | Medium | 1lb propane | Indoor-rated models only. Tip-over shutoff. Ventilate. |
| Candle heater (terra cotta pot method) | Low | Candles | Better than nothing. 4 tea candles under an inverted pot radiates heat. Fire risk — never leave unattended. |
| Body heat + insulation | Low | None | Sleeping bags, wool blankets, layers. In extremis, skin-to-skin contact inside a sleeping bag is the most effective field hypothermia treatment. |

**NEVER use for indoor heating:**
- Charcoal grill or barbecue (extreme CO producer)
- Gas oven (CO risk and gas leak risk)
- Unvented gas heater not rated for indoor use
- Car engine running in attached garage

## SOP 7.3: Cooling Without Power

**Heat illness kills faster than most people realize.** Core body temperature over 104°F = heat stroke = medical emergency.

**Strategies:**

1. **Stay in the lowest level of your home** — heat rises. Basements are naturally cooler.
2. **Cross-ventilation:** Open windows on opposite sides of the house. Hot air exits high; cool air enters low.
3. **Wet cloth cooling:** Soak a bandana or t-shirt in water, wear it. Evaporative cooling is your best friend. Place wet cloth on pulse points: neck, wrists, inside elbows, groin.
4. **Shade your windows externally** — hang sheets, tarps, or reflective material OUTSIDE the windows. Internal curtains trap heat between the glass and the room.
5. **Limit activity to dawn and dusk.** Rest during peak heat (10 AM - 4 PM).
6. **Hydrate aggressively.** Drink before you're thirsty. Add electrolytes to prevent hyponatremia (dangerous low sodium from excessive water without salt replacement).
7. **Improvised "AC":** Place a wet towel over an open window — incoming air is cooled by evaporation. This works best in low-humidity environments.

# MODULE 8: NAVIGATION WITHOUT GPS

## SOP 8.1: Compass Basics

**You need a baseplate compass** (Silva, Suunto, or equivalent — $15-30). Not a button compass, not a phone compass.

### Finding North Without a Compass

**Daytime — Shadow Method:**
1. Push a straight stick vertically into the ground
2. Mark the tip of the shadow with a rock (Point A)
3. Wait 15-20 minutes
4. Mark the new tip position (Point B)
5. A line from A to B runs roughly East-West. A is West, B is East.
6. A perpendicular line points North-South.

**Nighttime — North Star (Northern Hemisphere):**
1. Find the Big Dipper (Ursa Major)
2. The two stars forming the outer edge of the "cup" point to the North Star (Polaris)
3. Polaris is directly above true North

**Nighttime — Southern Cross (Southern Hemisphere):**
1. Find the Southern Cross constellation
2. Extend the long axis of the cross 4.5 times its length
3. Drop a line straight down to the horizon — that's roughly South

### Map Reading Essentials

**Buy physical topographic maps of your area NOW.** USGS 7.5-minute quadrangle maps are available free to download and print at the USGS website. Print them. Laminate them. Put them in your go-bag.

**Basic map reading:**
- **Contour lines** close together = steep terrain
- **Contour lines** far apart = gentle terrain
- Blue = water features
- Green = vegetation
- Brown = contour lines (elevation)
- Black = man-made features (roads, buildings)
- Red = major roads

**Orienting a map with a compass:**
1. Place compass on map
2. Rotate map until the compass needle (red end) aligns with the north arrows on the map
3. Your map is now oriented to the real world — features on the map match their real-world direction

# MODULE 9: COMMUNITY DEFENSE & ORGANIZATION

## SOP 9.1: The Neighborhood Alliance

*In an extended crisis, lone wolves die. Communities survive. Your first perimeter is your neighborhood.*

### The 48-Hour Window

In the first 48 hours of a widespread crisis, most people are still in shock, still hoping for a quick return to normal. This is your window to organize.

**Day 1 Actions:**
1. Make contact with immediate neighbors (both sides + across the street)
2. Share information only (what you know about the event, what you've heard on the radio)
3. Do NOT reveal your supply levels. Say "we're okay for now" and move on.
4. Identify:
   - Who has medical training? (Nurses, EMTs, doctors, combat medics, veterinarians)
   - Who has mechanical/construction skills?
   - Who has communication equipment (ham radio)?
   - Who has firearms experience and training?
   - Who has agricultural/gardening knowledge?
   - Who has young children or elderly who need extra support?

**Day 2 Actions:**
5. Call a meeting (front yard, neutral location)
6. Propose a simple structure:
   - Shared watch rotation (eyes on the street)
   - Information sharing (what have you heard?)
   - Resource pooling (only VOLUNTARILY — never pressure)
   - Mutual defense agreement (if someone's home is threatened, neighbors respond)

### Community Watch Protocol

**Minimum watch requirements:**
- Two people per shift (never alone — one watches, one is backup)
- 4-hour shifts maximum (fatigue = poor judgment)
- Communication device (radio or whistle system — 3 blasts = emergency)
- Observation point with good sight lines of approach routes
- Written log: Time, observation, action taken

**Escalation Levels:**

| Level | Situation | Response |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Green** | Normal conditions, no threats observed | Routine watch rotation |
| **Yellow** | Suspicious activity, unknown persons in area | Increase watch to 2 positions. Alert all households. |
| **Orange** | Direct threat observed (attempt to enter property, aggressive behavior) | All adults alert. Prepare to defend or evacuate. Attempt verbal de-escalation first. |
| **Red** | Active threat to life | Full community response. Defend property and persons. |

### Rules of Engagement (Critical)

1. **De-escalation first, always.** Most "threats" in a crisis are scared, desperate people, not predators. A calm voice and a firm boundary resolves 90% of confrontations.
2. **Warning before action.** "You need to leave this area now." Repeat clearly, loudly.
3. **Proportional response.** Match the level of force to the actual threat. A hungry person at your door is not the same as an armed group.
4. **No lone-wolf heroics.** If a situation requires force, it requires numbers. Wait for support.
5. **Document everything.** When this is over, there will be accountability. Write down what happened, when, who was involved, and what actions were taken.

# MODULE 10: THE BARTER ECONOMY

## SOP 10.1: Understanding Post-Crisis Economics

*Money doesn't disappear overnight, but its utility does. In a prolonged grid-down scenario, cash becomes increasingly useless as the things it buys become unavailable. Barter fills the gap.*

### The Barter Hierarchy of Needs

People will trade for what they need in this order:
1. **Water** (highest value in the first week)
2. **Food** (highest sustained value)
3. **Medicine** (always high value — even OTC pain meds)
4. **Fuel** (gasoline, propane, kerosene, firewood)
5. **Security** (ammunition, tools, services)
6. **Comfort** (alcohol, tobacco, coffee, candy, hygiene)
7. **Information** (maps, news, communication access)

### High-Value Barter Items to Stockpile NOW

**"Small Change" items (low cost, high trade value):**

| Item | Current Cost | Crisis Value | Why |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|
| 50ml "nip" bottles of liquor | $1-3 each | VERY HIGH | Morale, antiseptic, trading universal |
| Lighters (BIC disposable) | $1 each | HIGH | Fire is essential. Matches run out. |
| Travel-size hygiene (soap, shampoo) | $1 each | MODERATE | Hygiene prevents disease |
| OTC pain meds (individual dose packs) | $0.10/dose | HIGH | Pain relief is always in demand |
| Coffee (instant packets) | $0.25 each | HIGH | Caffeine withdrawal is real; morale |
| Batteries (AA, AAA) | $0.50 each | HIGH | Flashlights, radios need them |
| Duct tape (small rolls) | $3 each | MODERATE | Universal repair tool |
| Sewing kits | $1-3 each | MODERATE | Clothing repair |
| Hard candy | $0.05/piece | MODERATE | Morale, especially for children |
| Seeds (heirloom, non-GMO) | $2-3/pack | VERY HIGH (long crisis) | Food production = long-term survival |
| Salt | $1/lb | HIGH | Preservation, electrolytes, seasoning |

**"Large Denomination" items:**
- Ammunition (common calibers: 9mm, .22LR, 5.56, 12ga)
- Antibiotics (fish antibiotics: amoxicillin, ciprofloxacin)
- Solar panels / battery banks
- Water filters (Sawyer Squeeze — $20 now, priceless later)
- 1 oz silver rounds / "junk silver" (pre-1965 US coins — widely recognized, divisible)

### Barter Security Rules

1. **NEVER trade at your home.** Meet at a neutral location.
2. **NEVER go alone.** Bring at least one person who watches your back while you trade.
3. **NEVER reveal your supply depth.** "I have a little extra of this" — never "I have a closet full."
4. **NEVER trade weapons to people you don't know and trust.**
5. **Inspect everything.** Open cans could be spoiled. Medications could be expired. Water could be contaminated.
6. **Fair dealing builds reputation.** In a barter economy, your reputation IS your currency. Cheat someone and word spreads. Be known as fair and reliable.

# MODULE 11: PSYCHOLOGICAL RESILIENCE & CRISIS LEADERSHIP

*This may be the most important module in the Vault. All the tactical skills in the world are useless if your mind breaks first. In every historical disaster, the #1 predictor of survival is not gear, not training, not fitness — it is psychological resilience.*

## SOP 11.1: Understanding the Stress Response

### The 4 Phases of Crisis Psychology

**Phase 1: Impact (Hours 1-24)**
- Shock, disbelief, denial ("this can't be happening")
- Hyper-alertness OR freezing/shutdown
- Normal. Do not judge yourself or others for this response.
- **Action:** Focus on SOPs. Having a checklist to follow gives the brain something to DO, which breaks the freeze response. This is why you read Module 0 before a crisis.

**Phase 2: Recoil (Days 1-7)**
- Emotional swings: anger, fear, grief, numbness
- Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
- Sleep disruption, appetite changes
- Desire to help OR desire to flee
- **Action:** Maintain routine. Wake at the same time. Eat at scheduled times. Assign tasks. Routine is the scaffolding that holds your mind together when everything else falls apart.

**Phase 3: Reorganization (Weeks 1-4)**
- Accepting the "new normal"
- Problem-solving replaces panic
- Community bonds form or fracture
- Grief may deepen as the full scope becomes clear
- **Action:** Set daily goals. Small wins compound. "Today we improved the water filter." "Today we organized the watch schedule." Progress, even small, is the antidote to despair.

**Phase 4: Restoration (Months 1+)**
- New routines are established
- Purpose and meaning emerge
- Trauma processing begins
- Some people thrive; others develop PTSD
- **Action:** Talk about what happened. Telling your story to others is a primary trauma processing mechanism. Do not bottle it up. Do not tell others to "just move on."

## SOP 11.2: Managing Others Under Stress

### The STOP Protocol (For When Someone Is Panicking)

**S — Sit them down.** Physical positioning affects mental state. Standing + panic = running. Sitting = calming.

**T — Talk calmly and directly.** Low voice, slow pace, short sentences. "You are safe right now. I am here. We have a plan." Do NOT say "calm down" — it never works. Validate: "I know this is scary. It's okay to be scared."

**O — Orient them.** Give them one specific task. "I need you to count the water bottles and tell me how many we have." Task focus overrides panic. The task doesn't need to be important — it needs to engage their brain.

**P — Plan with them.** Once they're oriented, include them in the next step. "Here's what we're going to do in the next hour..." People who feel part of a plan feel less helpless.

### Children in Crisis

- **Ages 1-5:** They don't understand events but absorb parental emotion. If you're calm, they're calm. Maintain physical contact. Keep routines (meals, naps, bedtime stories — yes, even now).
- **Ages 6-12:** They understand something bad happened. Give age-appropriate honesty. "The power is out and it might be out for a while. We have food and water and a plan. Your job is [specific task]." Give them a role.
- **Ages 13-17:** Treat them as team members. They can handle more truth and more responsibility. Include them in planning. Idle teenagers in a crisis become anxious or reckless — keep them engaged.

**For all ages:**
- Maintain meals and sleep schedules as closely as possible
- Limit exposure to graphic scenes or distressing information
- Physical activity burns stress hormones — make them move (chores, exercise, play)
- Art, storytelling, and games are not luxuries — they are psychological necessities

# MODULE 12: CHILDREN, ELDERLY & SPECIAL NEEDS

## SOP 12.1: Infant & Toddler Considerations

**Formula/Breastfeeding:**
- If breastfeeding, mother needs 500 extra calories/day and AGGRESSIVE hydration
- If formula-feeding, stockpile formula (check expiry quarterly). Ready-to-feed is safest (no water contamination risk). Powdered formula must be mixed with purified water.
- **Do NOT use improvised "formula" recipes from the internet.** Nutritional imbalances can kill infants.

**Diapers:**
- Stockpile cloth diapers as backup. They're reusable, washable, and never expire.
- Diaper rash becomes a serious problem without running water. Stockpile zinc oxide cream.
- In extremis: absorbent cloth (t-shirt material), changed frequently, aired dry between uses

**Temperature regulation:**
- Infants cannot regulate body temperature well. They overheat and cool down faster than adults. In a heating emergency, keep them skin-to-skin against a parent's body inside clothing.

## SOP 12.2: Elderly Considerations

**Medication dependency:** Elderly household members often have critical daily medications with narrow margins. This is your #1 concern.
- Maintain a written list of: Drug name, dose, frequency, prescribing doctor, pharmacy
- 90-day supply minimum (work with doctor)
- Know which medications are TRULY life-critical (insulin, blood thinners, heart medications, seizure medications) vs. quality-of-life (cholesterol meds, etc.)

**Mobility:** Evacuation planning must account for limited mobility. Can they walk 1 mile? Climb stairs? Ride in a vehicle without a lift?

**Cognitive decline:** Confusion increases dramatically under stress, unfamiliar environments, and routine disruption. Gentle, patient, repetitive communication. Keep them in familiar surroundings when possible.

**Dehydration:** Elderly people have diminished thirst response. They will not drink enough on their own. Set a timer and offer water every 2 hours.

## SOP 12.3: Medical Device Dependencies

| Device | Power Need | Backup Plan |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| CPAP | 30-60W, 8h/night | Battery pack (Jackery 300 or similar, ~4 nights). Solar recharge. Manual airway positioning (elevation, side-sleeping). |
| Oxygen concentrator | 300-600W continuous | Limited battery backup. Stockpile portable O2 tanks. Contact home health provider about emergency supply. |
| Insulin pump | Low (battery) | Stockpile pump batteries. Have injectable insulin + syringes as backup. |
| Nebulizer | 100-150W | Handheld, manual models exist (no power required). Stockpile metered-dose inhalers as backup. |
| Motorized wheelchair | Rechargeable | Solar charging capable. Have a manual wheelchair available. Plan evacuation routes for wheelchair access. |
| Dialysis | Hospital-dependent | Know the location of every dialysis center within 100 miles. Have transportation plan. This is a true emergency without power — seek medical facility immediately. |

## SOP 13.1: The Air-Gapped Vault

This Vault should exist on a device with **no internet connectivity.** Here's why and how:

**Why:**
- In a cyber-attack scenario, connected devices can be compromised, encrypted (ransomware), or wiped
- Your survival information must be accessible regardless of internet/network status
- An offline copy cannot be remotely destroyed

**How:**
1. Obtain a dedicated USB drive or microSD card (128GB is more than sufficient)
2. Encrypt it with VeraCrypt (free, open-source, cross-platform) or BitLocker (Windows) or FileVault (Mac)
3. Copy the entire Vault to the encrypted drive
4. **Create THREE copies:**
   - Copy 1: In your go-bag
   - Copy 2: At your home, in a fireproof/waterproof safe
   - Copy 3: At your Bravo or Charlie rally point (cached at the secondary location)
5. Update all copies quarterly (when you do your Go-Bag Audit, SOP 0.2)

**Password Management:**
- Use a strong passphrase (4+ random words: "correct horse battery staple" style)
- Your household must memorize this passphrase. Write it on the inside cover of a physical book that you keep at home, if you must — but memorization is better.
- Do NOT use the same passphrase you use for anything online

# MODULE 14: SCENARIOS — FULL CRISIS WALKTHROUGHS

*These scenarios walk you through realistic multi-day crises with decision points. Read them BEFORE a crisis to build mental models. In an actual emergency, refer to the specific Module SOPs.*

## Scenario 14.1: Extended Power Grid Failure (Winter, Suburban)

**Setting:** February. You live in a suburban home in the northern US. A major ice storm has brought down power lines across three states. Estimated restoration: 7-14 days. Temperature: 15°F (-9°C) at night, 28°F (-2°C) during the day.

### Day 1 (Hour 0-24)

**6:00 AM:** Power goes out. You wake to a cold, dark house. Your phone has 73% battery.

**Immediate Actions (SOP 1.2):**
1. Check phone for information — local utility website confirms widespread outage, "multi-day restoration timeline"
2. Fill bathtubs with water (you don't know if pumping stations have backup power — they might not)
3. Close all interior doors to contain heat in one room
4. DO NOT open the freezer. It will maintain temperature for 48 hours.
5. Dress everyone in layers NOW while the house is still relatively warm (residual heat lasts 4-6 hours)

**Decision Point 1:** *Your gas fireplace has a standing pilot light and works without electricity. Your neighbor's house has no heat source. Their family has two young children. They knock on your door at 10 AM.*

**Analysis:** This is a community-building opportunity, not a threat. Invite them in. Body heat from 2 additional adults and 2 children in one room is a significant heat source. Sharing heat costs you nothing. You are building an alliance that will matter if this extends beyond 14 days.

**By noon:**
- Set up your "warm room" — smallest room with the fireplace. Hang blankets over doorways.
- Inventory food: You have ~10 days of food for your family of 3. With 4 additional people, that's ~4.3 days.
- Inventory water: Bathtubs full (80 gallons) + 6 gallons stored + water heater (40 gallons) = 126 gallons. At 1 gal/person/day for 7 people = 18 days. Water is fine.

**Decision Point 2:** *It's 3 PM. The sun is setting. You have a generator with 10 gallons of fuel. Do you start it?*

**Analysis:** No. Not yet. Save fuel for when you truly need it. On Day 1, your house still has residual heat, your food is still cold in the fridge/freezer, and your batteries are charged. The generator becomes critical on Day 3+ when batteries die and the freezer contents thaw.

**Evening:**
- Cook a hot meal using the gas stove (if you have one — gas often continues when electricity fails) or camp stove
- One hot meal per day is a morale imperative (SOP 5.2)
- Set up sleeping arrangements: Everyone in the warm room. Sleeping bags, blankets, couch cushions as mattresses
- Set a watch rotation: You and your neighbor alternate 4-hour shifts monitoring the fireplace and listening for sounds

### Day 3

**Situation:** Power is still out. House temperature in non-heated rooms is 38°F. Freezer has thawed — cook or lose the meat. Phone is dead. Roads are icy but passable.

**Morning Actions:**
1. Start the generator. Charge all phones and battery banks (2 hours of runtime).
2. Cook ALL remaining freezer meat on the grill — it's below freezing outside, so cooked meat stored outside in sealed containers will stay cold naturally (your yard is now your refrigerator).
3. Send your neighbor home to check their house (frozen pipes are a risk — they should check now).
4. Turn on the radio: NOAA reports restoration is on track for Day 10-14.

**Decision Point 3:** *The gas station 2 miles away has reopened with a generator running their pumps. The line is 30+ cars long. Do you go?*

**Analysis:** Yes, but not for gas. Go for information and to buy whatever supplies they have — batteries, food, propane. Bring cash (small bills). Leave one adult at home. Don't mention your generator to anyone in line. Fill up your vehicle (half-tank rule — yours is at 60%, so top it off). Buy 2 more 5-gallon gas cans if available.

### Day 7

**Situation:** You've used 15 gallons of fuel running the generator in 4-hour blocks every other day. You have 5 gallons remaining. Temperature dropped to 5°F last night. Water in the bathtub has a thin layer of ice on it.

**Key Decisions:**
- Shift to minimal generator use: 2 hours every 2 days, for charging only
- Fireplace is your primary heat and cooking source now. You need firewood. Your neighbor has a dead tree in their yard — cut it up (this is why a saw/axe matters).
- Water rationing: Reduce to 3/4 gallon per person per day. Melt snow to supplement (NOT a replacement — snow is 90% air, requiring enormous energy to melt for small water yields).

### Day 12

**Power returns.** Total days without grid: 12. You used 20 gallons of fuel, approximately 120 gallons of stored water, and consumed food stores worth approximately 28 days of single-person supply.

**After-Action Review:**
- What worked: Water storage (sufficient), community alliance (shared heat, shared watch), generator management (made fuel last)
- What didn't: Food calculations were tight — need to stockpile more for 7-person scenarios. Phone battery died too fast — need a larger solar panel for winter conditions.
- Action items: Buy 200W solar panel, increase food storage by 50%, get neighbor set up with their own go-bag

## Scenario 14.2: Rapid Urban Evacuation (Wildfire/Chemical Spill)

**Setting:** 2:00 PM on a Tuesday. You're at work, 12 miles from home. Your partner is home with your two children (ages 6 and 10). An emergency alert hits your phone: mandatory evacuation order for your area. You have an estimated 2-3 hours before the threat reaches your neighborhood.

### Hour 1: The Golden Hour

**Your Actions (at work, 12 miles away):**
1. Call/text your partner immediately: "Evacuation order. Execute SOP 1.3. I am coming home. ETA 30-45 minutes."
2. If you cannot reach them, trust the plan. Your partner has the FEP card and knows the rally points.
3. Leave work NOW. Do not wait for permission. Do not "wrap up one more thing."
4. Drive home via your pre-planned route. If main roads are already congested, take your secondary route.

**Your partner's actions (at home):**
1. Receives your message. Initiates the 10-Minute Evacuation Drill (SOP 1.3).
2. Children get their go-bags (pre-packed, staged in the closet).
3. Partner grabs: Go-bags, Vault drive, medications, cash, documents bag, pet carrier.
4. Vehicle loaded within 10 minutes.
5. If you arrive home in time: depart together.
6. If you DO NOT arrive within 45 minutes: Partner departs with children to Rally Point Bravo and leaves the pre-arranged signal at your Alpha point (note under doormat: "Left at [time], heading to Bravo").

**Decision Point:** *Traffic is at a standstill on the main evacuation route. Your GPS shows the highway is a parking lot. You're 4 miles from home with an estimated 90-minute drive time due to traffic.*

**Analysis:**
- 4 miles is walkable in 60-75 minutes. Drivable in 90+ minutes in this traffic.
- If you walk, you abandon your vehicle (a major resource).
- Best option: Take side streets. Residential neighborhoods will be less congested than arterial roads. Your physical map shows an alternate route through neighborhood streets. It's longer in distance (6 miles) but faster in time (25-30 minutes by car).
- This is why you pre-planned multiple routes. This is why you carry physical maps.

### Hour 2: Departure

**You arrive home.** Partner has vehicle loaded and ready. Children are in the car with their go-bags.

**Final 5-minute actions before departing:**
1. Shut off gas at meter
2. Shut off water main
3. Close all windows and doors
4. Take a video walkthrough of every room (30 seconds per room) for insurance documentation
5. Leave

**Route to Rally Point Charlie (50 miles away, outside the evacuation zone):**
- Primary route: Highway south (currently gridlocked — skip)
- Secondary route: County roads west, then south — longer but moving
- Monitor radio for updated evacuation routes and road closures

**Decision Point:** *You're on your secondary route. You have 3/4 tank of gas (enough for 300+ miles). A neighbor from two streets over flags you down — she's elderly, alone, and has no vehicle. Taking her means squeezing 6 people into a 5-seat car.*

**Analysis:** Take her. She has critical medications in her bag. Her survival depends on this. A slightly overcrowded car for 50 miles is trivially inconvenient compared to leaving an elderly person in an evacuation zone. Buckle the 6-year-old in the middle with lap belt, elderly person in the remaining seat. It's not ideal, but it's a crisis — you optimize for life, not traffic law compliance.

**Setting:** A severe pandemic has resulted in: 30% workforce absent, supply chain disruptions leading to sporadic grocery availability, schools closed, work-from-home orders for most non-essential workers. Unlike a sudden event, this is a slow squeeze over weeks.

### Week 1: Early Warning

- Reports of a novel pathogen in another country: "Probably nothing." (It's never nothing.)
- First domestic cases confirmed: "Only 10 cases." (If you can see 10, there are 1,000.)
- WHO/CDC begins "monitoring": Start buying supplies. Don't wait for recommendations.

**Immediate Actions (Day 1 of your awareness):**
1. Cash withdrawal: $1,000-2,000 in small bills. Banks may limit withdrawals or close.
2. Grocery run — buy shelf-stable food for 60 days per person (see Module 5 calorie calculator):
   - Rice: 50 lbs ($20)
   - Beans: 30 lbs ($25)
   - Oats: 20 lbs ($15)
   - Canned goods: 60 cans assorted ($60)
   - Peanut butter: 10 jars ($25)
   - Cooking oil: 5 bottles ($20)
   - Salt, sugar, honey, spices ($30)
   - **Total: ~$200 for 60 days for 2 adults**
3. Pharmacy run: 90-day supply of all prescriptions (if your insurance allows). OTC medication kit (Module 3).
4. Fuel: Fill all vehicles. Fill gas cans.
5. Hygiene: Bleach, soap, sanitizer, toilet paper, trash bags, gloves, N95 masks.
6. **Do all of this in ONE trip.** Do not make multiple trips over multiple days. The window closes fast.

### Week 2-3: The Squeeze

**Situation:** Grocery stores are operating but with limited hours and bare shelves in key categories (meat, dairy, cleaning supplies, toilet paper). Prices have increased 30-50% on available items. Online delivery is booked 2-3 weeks out.

**Your position (if you prepped in Week 1):** You're fine. You have 60 days of food. You are not in lines. You are not exposing yourself unnecessarily.

**Actions:**
- Continue monitoring news/radio for escalation or de-escalation signals
- Begin rationing to extend your runway (reduce from 2,000 to 1,600 cal/day)
- If you have a garden space, plant NOW (Module 5 fast-growing crops)
- Maintain social connections via phone/video — isolation is psychologically corrosive
- Help neighbors who are struggling with information and small supplies (not your core stock)

### Week 4-8: New Normal

**Potential escalations to monitor:**
- Power grid instability (reduced workforce at power plants) → Trigger Module 6
- Water system failures (reduced workforce at treatment plants) → Trigger Module 2
- Civil unrest (desperation, political tension) → Trigger Module 9
- Secondary health crisis (people avoiding hospitals for non-COVID emergencies = deaths from untreated conditions)

|------|----------|

| 7:00 AM | Breakfast, morning briefing (radio, news scan) |
| 8:00 AM | Work/school (remote) |
